Civil War's conflict is actually a two in one.

The primary conflict is basically whether or not to enlist into what would essentially be an officially government sanctioned super hero team.

This would mean that at the highest level, the UN would get to determine who the Avengers fight, when, and where. On the lowest level, it would also mean that anyone discovered to have power's would need to register in order to be allowed to operate at all. It's a way to legitimize vigilantes.

Iron Man believes this is the right course as it means they can be held accountable for how dangerous they are plus it means that the government can technically be responsible for collateral damage as well. It's really not a bad way to go for heroes.

Except, Captain America points out that this means that the Avengers have to check in with their bosses before doing their thing. Part of the fear is the bureaucracy slowing then down from jumping into action. The other fear is that if the government decides that certain people in trouble are not worth saving for whatever reason, then Avengers would have to stand by.

Captain America believes heroes need to be able to operate freely in order to do the most good, with the unspoken rule that other heroes would be there to police them.

The secondary conflict however is about what to do with Bucky.

Tony thinks Bucky should pay for what he did and doesn't care that Bucky can't remember it or was under mind control. And he feels betrayed when Steve defends Bucky.
Black Panther is sort of in agreement until he realizes that Baron Zemo is the true enemy. 

On this front, Steve really cares more about Bucky being his friend and having faith that he can be redeemed, having seen that there still some semblance of original Bucky in there.
